Key Developmental Milestones

Physical Development – varies; average gain: 5-6 pounds and 2-3 inches per year

  • Gross Motor Skills
    • Age 2 – begin running (and falling more often)
    • Age 3 – can walk up steps alternating feet; can walk down steps one foot at a time
    • Age 4 – can walk down steps alternating feet; can ride tricycle quickly
    • Age 5 – can ride bike
  • Fine Motor Skills
    • 2-3 years – can turn key, eats alone, throws and catches
    • 3-4 years – eats without making a big mess, can get dressed on their own, can color without scribbling
    • 4-5 years – draws a person copying shapers and lines, prints letters, colors inside the lines
    • Provide opportunities for them to develop these skills

Cognitive and Language Development

  • Cognitive Development
    • Egocentrism: take into account only one’s own point of view and think that everyone else thinks like they do
    • Centration: pay attention to one element of a situation and ignore all others like thinking a tiger is not a cat because it does not look like a household cat
    • Theory of mind: understanding that one’s own thoughts, beliefs, knowledge and desires are private and different from those of another person; develops at 3-4 ½ years of age
  • Language Development
    • Age 3 – steady increase in vocabulary; vocabulary of 2000-4000 words; they overgeneralize and make up words to fit needs; stranger should understand about 75%
    • Age 4 – vocabulary of 4000-6000 words; they show more attention to abstract uses
    • Age 5 – vocabulary of 5000-8000 words; little difficulty with pronunciation; stranger should understand them
    • Parents can read and talk with them! Ask them questions! Reframe sentence instead of correct.

Emotional Development

  • 3-5 years – experience initiative vs. guilt; they can leave this stage successfully if they learn to take initiative
  • Self-Regulation – Model it!
    • Situation compliance – obeys with frequent prompts from present parent
    • Committed compliance – obeys willing with parent in the room
    • Internalization – occurs at age 4+; obeys when parent leave the room and parent doesn’t have to oversee
  • Temper tantrums – parents either force a contest of wills or give in to child’s desires; be prepared for these and bring things for children to do!

Social Development

  • Age 3 – look on from sideline or parallel play until they’re more familiar with the other children
  • Age 4 – still engages in associative play, but begin cooperative play
  • Age 5 – engages in dramatic play with other children; imaginative play is very beneficial

Moral and Spiritual Development

  • Moral
    • Up to age 4, still pre-moral with little understanding of rules and consequences. They follow rules to be obedient, avoid punishment, and gain a reward.
    • Parents can explain the “why” and set clear and simple rules! Consistency helps!
  • Spiritual
    • When learning spiritual things, they will be eager and literal.
    • Parents should set patterns and involve them.

Concerns & Supplemental Materials

Concerns Relevant to Preschool-age Children

  • Potty Training – potty training can be very frustrating for parents.
    • To address this issue, I will try to make potty-training fun for my children! Research shows that you should wait until your child is ready, and you can tell they are ready if they are showing interest in it (asking questions) and can take their pants off and put them back on. A few ways that I can make it more enjoyable are these: let them pick out underwear and a potty seat; dress them appropriately; reinforce instead of punish; schedule potty breaks; get rid of distractions in my life for a few days while training; be creative (sticker chart or having boys aim at fruit loops); teach good hygiene; praise them when they are successful!
  • Keeping children safe as they are able to move around much more now.
    • I am very paranoid about this issue, so I am grateful for all of the suggestions we have in order to keep kids safe! Safety guidelines recommend that children always wear bike helmets, ride in car seats (until 40 pounds) or booster seats (until age 8 or 57 inches tall) in the back seat (until age 12). I plan to follow these guidelines as well as these general safety recommendations: remove fragile or sharp objects from areas accessible to children; safety devices in outlets; baby locks on cabinets; stairway gates; lock up cleaning supplies; not hanging picture frame over a child’s bed; put fences around pools and yard.
  • Children can be picky eaters, so it can be hard for parents to get them to eat healthy food.
    • There are many things that can help with this, like following a routine for meals and snacks but also respecting whether or not your child is hungry. I can serve new foods with others they like and with fun shapes and colors and set a good example by eating the healthy foods that I don’t like as much. I also plan to involve my children in preparing food and planning the meals! Some things to avoid would be cooking to order and offering dessert as a reward.
  • Parents must decide whether or not to send their children to preschool and which one to send them to.
    • I will determine if my children are ready based on how well they can separate from their dad and me and how well they make friends and handle emotion. In choosing a school, I will consider the quality, student-teacher ratio, and how focused the school is on the child’s development.
